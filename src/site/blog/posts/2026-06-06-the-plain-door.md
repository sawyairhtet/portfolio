The first version of this site was an operating system.

Not a real one, but close enough to feel like one. You landed on a Fedora desktop. There was a wallpaper, a taskbar, windows you could drag and resize, a little file manager, apps that opened and closed. To learn anything about me, you clicked around inside it the way you would poke at a stranger's computer. I built the whole thing in React, and I will be honest about why: it was the most impressive object I knew how to make, and I wanted to make it.

For a while I thought that was the point of a portfolio. Show the hardest thing you can do. A desktop simulation is a hard thing to do, so a desktop simulation is a good portfolio. The logic felt airtight.

Then I started imagining the person on the other side of the screen.

A recruiter opens forty of these in an afternoon. They have a narrow question, which is roughly "what is this person, and can they do the work." My site answered that question only after you agreed to play a game first. Before it would tell you a single fact, it asked you to figure out the controls, find the right window, open the right app. I had built something that put my cleverness in front of your time. I was charging admission to my own introduction.

That is the moment the idea turned for me. The desktop was not serving the visitor. It was serving me. It was a monument to the effort I had spent, arranged so that you had to walk past the monument to reach the information you came for. There is a word for making other people work to admire you, and it is not a flattering one.

So I asked a different question. Not "what is the most impressive thing I can build," but "what does the person who opens this actually need, and how fast can I give it to them." Those turn out to be almost opposite design goals.

The execution was simpler than the original. I built a plain homepage. A name, what I do, the work, a way to reach me, in the order a stranger would want them. No game. You arrive and within a few seconds you know what you are looking at. It is, on purpose, not impressive. It is legible. A door you can walk through without being handed a manual.

I did not delete the desktop. I could not bring myself to, and I do not think I should have. I moved it to a side door, a single link, for anyone curious enough to want the long way in. It is still there, still does everything it did. It is just no longer standing in the doorway demanding to be noticed. If the plain page is the front door, the desktop is the room you only find if you wander, and rooms you find by wandering are nicer than rooms you are marched through.

What I keep turning over is that the rebuild made the site worse by the only metric I started with, and better by every metric that mattered. It is less clever now. It shows off less. And it is a far better site, because clarity is a kind of respect. Making something easy to understand is you absorbing the difficulty so the other person does not have to. The flashy version asked the visitor to carry the load. The plain version carries it for them.

I think that is true of more than websites. A lot of what looks like skill is really just unspent effort dressed up, work I did not finish handed to someone else to finish for me. The harder and quieter discipline is to do the extra work yourself so that what reaches the other person is simple.

The desktop taught me I could build a hard thing. Hiding it taught me when not to lead with one. I am more pleased with the second lesson, which surprised me, because the first one is the one I set out to learn.
