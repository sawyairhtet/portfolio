About Me

The story behind the code and the camera

I'm a developer who fell in love with photography, and a photographer who can't stop coding. I spend my days building web applications that solve real problems and my evenings chasing perfect light with a camera. Sometimes these worlds collide in the most beautiful ways.

How I Got Here

It started with a simple problem: I had thousands of photos scattered across different devices and needed a way to organize them. Instead of buying software, I decided to build my own. That first Python script opened my eyes to how code could enhance creativity rather than replace it.

While studying computer science, I picked up my first real camera – a used Fujifilm X-T2. The precision required in photography reminded me of programming: every setting matters, composition follows rules (that can be broken), and the difference between good and great often lies in the details most people never notice.

Today, I work as a full-stack developer by day and shoot portraits, events, and street photography by night and weekends. But the real magic happens when these worlds intersect – building tools for photographers, creating web experiences that showcase visual work beautifully, or using code to push the boundaries of digital art.

What I Do

I build web applications with React, Node.js, and Python. I love creating tools that help creative professionals manage their work – portfolio sites, client galleries, booking systems, and image processing APIs.

How I Work

Details Matter

Whether it's pixel-perfect UI components or perfectly exposed photos, I believe excellence lives in the details that most people never consciously notice.

Tools Should Disappear

The best technology gets out of the way and lets people focus on what they're creating. I build interfaces that feel intuitive and cameras that become extensions of vision.

Always Learning

Both fields evolve constantly. I read code, study other photographers' work, experiment with new techniques, and stay curious about what's possible.

Human-Centered

Technology should amplify human creativity, not replace it. Every project I work on starts with understanding the people who will use it.
